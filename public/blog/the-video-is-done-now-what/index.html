The video is done. Now what?

Jul 14, 2026 / Marketing / 5 min read

You wrapped. The footage is graded, the cut is locked, the client signed off. Here is the part nobody warns you about: finishing the video is the halfway point, not the finish line.

I have watched too many good videos die in a folder. A polished piece that nobody sees is an expensive home movie. The shoot was the easy part. Getting it in front of the right people, over and over, is where most of the value actually lives, and it is the step small businesses skip the most.

So before you move on to the next thing, here is the distribution playbook I walk every client through the day we deliver.

01Cut it for the room it plays in

A video built for the top of your website is not the same video that works in a phone feed. The homepage version can breathe. The feed version cannot. It needs to be vertical or square, it needs captions burned in because most people watch with the sound off, and it needs the hook in the first two seconds.

Ask for those cutdowns while the project is still open on the edit timeline. Going back a month later to re-export for a new platform costs more, takes longer, and it always shows. One master, several native cuts. Plan for it up front.

02The first frame is the ad

Your thumbnail and your opening frame do more selling than the other fifty-nine seconds combined. Pick a frame with a face, motion, or a clear promise. If someone scrolling has to guess what they are looking at, they have already scrolled past.

This is a place where the craft and the marketing meet. A good DP is already thinking about the frame that stops a thumb, not just the frame that wins an award.

03Post it where your customers already are

You do not need to be on every platform. You need to be on the one or two where your actual buyers spend their time. A local service business and a national product brand should not have the same posting map, and copying a competitor's channel mix is how you end up shouting into an empty room.

Pick the platforms on purpose. Then post the video natively on each one instead of dropping a link. Every feed rewards video you upload directly and buries links that send people away.

04One shoot, ten posts

A single shoot day should feed you for a month, not a single afternoon. The finished piece is the hero, but it is not the only thing you paid for.

  • The full cut for your site and your pinned post.
  • Three to five short clips pulled from the best moments.
  • A handful of stills for graphics, email, and thumbnails.
  • A behind-the-scenes clip, because people buy from people.
  • The single best line of dialogue as a quote card.

Tell your videographer you want the project cut for repurposing before the camera rolls. Good coverage on set is what makes ten posts possible later. Thin coverage is what leaves you with one clip and nothing to say next week.

Views are vanity. Track the number that shows up in your bank account.

05Measure the thing that pays you

It is easy to fall in love with the view count. Views feel like a scoreboard. But a hundred thousand views that book zero jobs is worse than a thousand views that book three, and the small number is the one that keeps the lights on.

Decide what a win looks like before you post. Calls booked, forms filled, demos scheduled, seats sold. Put a way to take that action right next to the video, every single time. Then watch the number that actually pays you, and let it tell you what to make next.

None of this requires a bigger budget. It requires deciding, before the camera is even packed, where the video is going to live and who needs to see it. Get that right and an ordinary video outperforms a beautiful one that nobody found.
